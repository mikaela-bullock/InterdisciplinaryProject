About & Motivation

My name is Mikaela Bullock. I am a student at the University of Wisconsin–Madison majoring in Economics (Math Emphasis) and Computer Science.

I care about answering this question because it reflects my own position as a student who is involved in both worlds. As a computer science and economics major who spends a lot of time reading and writing, I constantly feel the gap between how my STEM classes and humanities-oriented work define “expertise.” In programming courses, clarity often means efficiency and correctness, while in humanities clarity is about nuance and the ability to account for multiple perspectives.

This project is a way for me to express these tensions rather than just living with them. By building my own website, I can use my technical skills in HTML and CSS while drawing on the interpretive and rhetorical skills I have practiced in this course. Digital design is inherently interdisciplinary, and I want this project’s form to reflect that message.

Course Question

What is the relationship between STEM and Humanities thinking and expertise?

I explore this question by examining how each discipline defines clarity, what counts as strong communication, and how meaning is produced not just through information, but through presentation, interpretation, and context.

My Argument

My argument is that STEM and Humanities expertise are not opposites but interdependent. Technical knowledge becomes meaningful when it can be communicated, interpreted, and ethically framed. Likewise, humanistic analysis gains power when it engages with technical systems, data, and the tools that shape modern life.

In my academic experience, STEM disciplines often prioritize efficiency and correctness, while humanities disciplines emphasize ambiguity, perspective, and audience. This project argues that true expertise requires both precision and interpretive depth.

Artifacts (from different disciplines)

Artifact 1: This Website (HTML/CSS)

My first artifact is the website itself, which I coded using HTML and CSS. The site functions as both a project and an argument: neither STEM nor the humanities can stand alone. Without humanities-based skills like organization, rhetorical awareness, and clarity of expression, I would not be able to communicate my technical work in a meaningful way. It would be unfair of me to assume that everyone has a strong technical background, so it is important for me to be skilled at articulating myself to those who may not understand tech jargon.

Artifact 2: Art of Science Exhibition

My second artifact is the Art of Science exhibition discussed in class. It exemplifies the relationship between scientific inquiry and artistic interpretation by transforming data, experimentation, and observation into visual forms that invite reflection and engagement. This image strengthens my argument by demonstrating that scientific discovery depends on interpretive and communicative choices traditionally associated with the humanities. While the image documents a biological breakthrough, its meaning emerges through visual design decisions such as color enhancement, framing, and emphasis. These choices guide the viewer’s understanding and transform technical data into accessible knowledge. In this way, the image mirrors the structure of my own project: both rely on technical expertise to produce content, but require rhetorical and visual interpretation to make that content meaningful.

Sources

The following sources provide theoretical, historical, and educational perspectives that deepen my understanding of the relationship between STEM and humanities expertise. Each source helps explain why interdisciplinary thinking is not supplemental, but essential to meaningful communication.

  1. Ken Arnold: “A very public affair: art meets science.”
    https://journals-sagepub-com.ezproxy.library.wisc.edu/doi/pdf/10.1080/03080188.2017.1381224

    Ken Arnold’s analysis of art–science collaborations helps clarify why visual forms like the Art of Science exhibition are central to how scientific knowledge becomes meaningful. Arnold argues that interdisciplinary work is most powerful when it exists in public-facing forms, noting that “interdisciplinary collisions are most evocatively and meaningfully encountered by looking at things in public galleries.” This claim is important to my argument because it frames exhibitions and visual artifacts as active sites of knowledge production rather than mere illustrations of scientific facts that exist in isolation. This connects well with The Art of Science Exhibition because it is exactly the type of process Arnold refers to: although it documents a biological breakthrough, its significance emerges through visual emphasis, color, and framing that guide interpretation. Arnold’s work reinforces this idea that STEM expertise depends on humanities-based practices such as visual rhetoric and audience awareness to transform technical discoveries into shared, public knowledge.

  2. Art Institute of Chicago. “The Enduring Relationship of Science and Art”
    https://archive.artic.edu/sciarttech/2a1.html

    The Art Institute of Chicago’s discussion of art and scientific investigation provides a historical foundation for my argument that STEM and humanities thinking have never been separate domains. The text states that “science and art naturally overlap” and emphasizes that both disciplines are “means of investigation” in which ideas and hypotheses are tested through the collaboration of mind and hand. This claim is significant because it challenges the modern assumption that technical and expressive expertise belong to different intellectual spheres. By tracing this overlap from ancient Greek concepts of techne through figures like Leonardo da Vinci, Monet, and Picasso, the source demonstrates that scientific precision and artistic interpretation have long worked together to produce knowledge. This historical perspective strengthens my argument by showing that interdisciplinary thinking is not a contemporary trend but a foundational mode of inquiry that continues to shape how complex ideas are understood and communicated today.

  3. “Fusion of Biology and Art: .” The American Biology Teacher.
    https://bioone-org.ezproxy.library.wisc.edu/journals/the-american-biology-teacher/volume-87/issue-3/abt.2025.87.3.159/

    The interdisciplinary biology education study “The Visual Art of Biology” further supports my argument that scientific understanding deepens when technical knowledge is paired with creative and expressive practices. The authors emphasize that “science helps create citizens who question the why and how of things,” but argue that this goal is best achieved through active, interdisciplinary learning rather than rote memorization. This claim is significant because it reframes science education as a process of inquiry and interpretation, not just information acquisition. By showing that students better comprehend biological concepts when they translate them into artistic forms, the study demonstrates how humanities-based skills such as visual communication, reflection, and meaning-making enhance scientific expertise. This directly parallels my own project: while I rely on technical coding skills to construct the website, it is the interpretive and rhetorical choices—developed through humanities coursework—that allow my argument about STEM and humanities to become accessible and compelling.
